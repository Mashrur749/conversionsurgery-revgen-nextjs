### Q: Do I need a permit to finish my basement in Calgary?

**AI response:** Yes — a building permit is required for any basement development in Calgary. ### Q: Can I add a legal secondary suite to my house?

**AI response:** Possibly — it depends on your property&apos;s zoning and your home&apos;s layout. Most Calgary residential zones (R-1, R-C1, R-C1L, R-C2, and others) permit secondary suites, but it&apos;s worth confirming your specific address on the City of Calgary&apos;s website before assuming. The physical requirements are the main constraint: the suite needs to be self-contained (its own kitchen, bathroom, sleeping area, and entrance accessible from outside without going through the main unit), it can&apos;t exceed 70 m&sup2; in floor area, and it has to meet the Alberta Building Code for fire separation, ceiling height (minimum 1.95m), and egress windows in bedrooms. Whether your home is a good candidate comes down to the layout and what&apos;s already roughed in. That&apos;s exactly what I look at on a site visit — I can usually tell you within 30 minutes whether a legal suite is realistic and what the path looks like.

### Q: What are the City of Calgary requirements for a legal secondary suite?

**AI response:** There are two layers: the development permit requirements (City of Calgary Land Use Bylaw) and the building code requirements (Alberta Building Code). Here&apos;s the short version of what the City requires: the suite must be self-contained with its own kitchen, living area, sleeping area, and bathroom; there must be an entrance accessible from outside without passing through the main unit; the suite can&apos;t exceed 70 m&sup2; floor area; and there&apos;s typically a requirement for one additional off-street parking stall. On the building code side, the main things are: fire separation between the suite and the main dwelling (minimum 30-minute fire resistance rating on walls and ceiling), smoke and CO detectors, egress windows in all bedrooms (minimum 0.35 m&sup2; opening, no dimension less than 380 mm), and minimum ceiling height of 1.95m throughout. The suite also needs its own electrical panel sub-feed and separate smoke detection. For the official and most current requirements, the City of Calgary&apos;s secondary suites page at calgary.ca/suites is the authoritative source — requirements do get updated. I can walk you through all of this at the site visit and show you what it means for your specific home.

**Customization needed:** None required. This is accurate as of 2025-2026 Calgary requirements.

### Q: Can I put the suite on Airbnb?

**AI response:** You can, but there are rules specific to Calgary that are worth knowing before you go that route. As of April 2025, Calgary requires a short-term rental business licence for any rental under 180 days. There are two categories: Primary Residence (around $286 all-in including fire inspection fee) and Non-Primary Residence (around $624). Your suite also needs to be a legal secondary suite to operate as a short-term rental — illegal suites can&apos;t be licensed. On the insurance side, you&apos;ll need liability coverage of at least $2 million issued by an Alberta-registered insurer. The City also requires a floor plan showing all rented spaces, smoke alarms, fire extinguishers, and emergency exits. The short-term rental rules are managed separately from the suite permit — you&apos;d deal with those after the suite is built and registered. For current licensing details, the City of Calgary&apos;s short-term rental page is the place to check, since these rules were recently updated and may continue to change. We build the suite to code; the licensing and operating decisions are yours to manage on the rental side.

### Q: I&apos;m worried about moisture in my basement — is that something you deal with?

**AI response:** It&apos;s a smart thing to think about before finishing a basement, especially in Calgary. Our climate is harder on foundations than people realize — the freeze-thaw cycles we get in spring and fall put real stress on concrete and can work existing cracks wider over time. Calgary&apos;s clay-heavy soil also retains moisture long after rain or snowmelt, which means foundations are regularly under hydrostatic pressure. Here&apos;s how we handle it: before we frame anything, we do a moisture assessment of the space. If there&apos;s visible efflorescence (white mineral staining on the walls), active seepage, or evidence of past water intrusion, we flag it. We can address minor interior moisture issues as part of the development — vapour barriers, drainage membrane on the floor, proper insulation detailing. What we don&apos;t do is cover up a water problem with drywall. If the basement has active water intrusion coming through the foundation walls or floor slab, that needs exterior waterproofing before we start, and we&apos;ll tell you that plainly. We&apos;d rather refer you to a foundation specialist first than have you redo the whole basement in three years. Once the envelope is solid, your finished basement should last decades.

**Customization needed:** None required. Optional: if the contractor has a partnership with a waterproofing or foundation repair company they regularly refer to, name them here.

### Q: My basement has low ceilings — can you still finish it?

**AI response:** Depends on how low. Alberta Building Code requires a minimum ceiling height of 1.95m (about 6&apos;4&quot;) for habitable space. Bedrooms in a legal suite actually need 2.13m (7&apos;0&quot;) to meet residential occupancy requirements. If you&apos;re at or just under those numbers, there&apos;s usually some engineering we can do — sometimes just addressing the floor system or mechanical runs can recover a few inches. If you&apos;re significantly under, the options are either benching or underpinning. Benching builds a concrete ledge inside the existing footings — it&apos;s faster and 20-40% cheaper than underpinning, but it reduces usable floor area. Underpinning lowers the entire slab by excavating below the existing footings and pouring new ones — it&apos;s more expensive (typically $50,000–$80,000 for a 1,000 sq ft basement on its own), but it preserves the full floor area and can add 2–4 feet of height. Both require engineering and permits. If your ceiling is under code height, I&apos;d want to measure properly at the site visit before telling you which direction makes sense — it depends on the joist depth, what&apos;s running through the ceiling, and your goals for the space.

**Customization needed:** None required. Optional: if the contractor offers benching or underpinning as a service, add a sentence confirming that. If they refer that work out, say so.
