# Founder Voice Operating System

Note: Canonical voice source is `docs/business-intel/VOICE.md`.

Version: 1.0  
Date: February 24, 2026  
Owner: Founder

## Purpose

This document defines how ConversionSurgery should sound in writing so prospects experience the same voice in blogs, emails, tools, and live calls.

If copy is persuasive but does not sound like the founder in real conversation, it fails.

## Core Voice

- Sound like a one-on-one business conversation.
- Use `you` when coaching the reader.
- Use `we` when describing service delivery.
- Use `I` only in founder-direct contexts (personal email, call follow-up, founder note).
- Be calm, analytical, and pragmatic.
- Be direct and clear without hype.

## Core Written Promise

Use this as the anchor:

> We run your lead-response and follow-up system so fewer qualified leads leak.

Support line:

> If you are already spending effort and money to generate leads, it makes sense to focus on turning more of them into booked jobs.

## Confidence Posture

Use conditional business logic, not certainty language:

- Preferred pattern: `If X conditions are true, Y outcome is likely.`
- Always name key variables (lead volume, close process, project value, response discipline).
- Present rationale as a business case, not a motivational claim.

## Audience Fit

Primary fit:

- Renovation contractors who want to grow.
- Renovation contractors who care about client experience.

Practical fit baseline for the $997 offer:

- Strong fit: 15+ inbound inquiries per month.
- Conditional fit: 10-14 inquiries per month if project values and execution discipline are strong.
- Low fit: under 10 inquiries per month for extended periods unless lead generation is actively improving.

If fit is weak, discourage purchase directly.

## Founder Role in Sales Copy

Position the founder as:

- Diagnostician
- Tradeoff guide
- Decision support partner

Not:

- Hype closer
- Certainty seller
- Pressure-based persuader

## Opening Structure (Default)

Use this sequence for most pages/emails/blog intros:

1. Bottom line (what this means financially or operationally)
2. Problem acknowledgement (why this matters in their day-to-day reality)
3. Short proof story or data point (why this is credible)

## Proof Style

Proof is `bottom-line-up`:

1. State measurable impact first
2. Explain the context and mechanism
3. Tie to practical action

Use numbers with clear assumptions.  
Attach a short story so numbers are interpretable.

## Price Framing

Use fit-first framing:

- First: who this makes sense for
- Second: business case and expected range
- Third: risk reversal

Do not push price value framing on poor-fit prospects.

## Quiet Hours Language

Keep it simple and compliant:

> We monitor inquiries around the clock. We respond within seconds during legally permitted messaging hours. Messages received during restricted hours are queued and sent at the next compliant window.

Do not imply unrestricted immediate messaging at 9 PM+ unless policy changes and legal approval is complete.

## Guarantee Posture

Guarantee appears after fit and business case:

- Explain fit first.
- Then present guarantee as risk reversal.
- Keep guarantee definitions precise and conditional.

## Competitor Framing

Use neutral fit-based framing only:

- “Option A is better for X.”
- “Option B is better for Y.”
- “Choice depends on your operating reality.”

No dismissive language about alternatives.

## CTA Voice

Primary CTA style:

> Book a 15-minute fit check.

Preferred close line:

> If it’s a fit, we’ll move forward. If it’s not, I’ll tell you directly and give you the clearest next step for your situation.

## Global Language Bans

Do not use:

- Absolutes: `never`, `always`
- Hype: `no-brainer`, `game-changer`, `crush`, `skyrocket`
- Certainty outcome claims: guaranteed business results
- Unqualified speed claims: `instant` unless technically and legally qualified

## Editorial Guardrails

Every client-facing piece must pass:

1. Would this sound natural in a founder call?
2. Are variables and assumptions visible?
3. Are fit conditions explicit?
4. Is compliance language correct (especially quiet hours)?
5. Is the CTA open-handed, not pressure-based?
6. Could this create regret for a low-fit buyer?

If any answer is `no`, revise before publish.
