# ConversionSurgery — Sales Objection Playbook

Version 1.0
Date: April 1, 2026
Audience: Solo operator running sales conversations
Purpose: Exact scripts for every objection a renovation contractor raises. Use the right response verbatim or close to it. Do not improvise on the wrong response column.

---

## HOW TO USE THIS PLAYBOOK

Each objection follows this structure:

- **The objection** — the contractor's actual words
- **Why they say it** — the fear underneath the words
- **The wrong response** — what not to say (and why it backfires)
- **The right response** — the exact script
- **Proof point** — what to show or demo in the moment

Tier 1 objections (10/10 and 7/10 agent consensus) will come up in almost every conversation. Know them cold. Tier 2 (5/10) are common. Tier 3 are likely with specific contractor profiles.

### Words to Avoid in All Sales Conversations

| Never say | Say instead | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| &ldquo;Automated follow-up&rdquo; | &ldquo;Your leads get followed up on even when you&apos;re mid-job&rdquo; | &ldquo;Automated&rdquo; sounds like a chatbot they&apos;ve been burned by |
| &ldquo;AI platform&rdquo; or &ldquo;AI tool&rdquo; | &ldquo;The system&rdquo; or &ldquo;your response team&rdquo; | &ldquo;AI&rdquo; triggers skepticism. Lead with outcomes. |
| &ldquo;You need to fix your follow-up&rdquo; | &ldquo;You can&apos;t be texting leads from a roof &mdash; that&apos;s a capacity problem, not a you problem&rdquo; | Never imply they&apos;re doing something wrong |
| &ldquo;Our automation handles it&rdquo; | &ldquo;We handle it&rdquo; | They&apos;re buying a service, not software |

**The line that stops contractors cold:** &ldquo;You built the estimate. You shouldn&apos;t have to beg for the job.&rdquo;

**Guarantee before price, every time.** State the guarantee and month-to-month terms before you say $1,000. Always.

## TIER 1 — HANDLE THESE EVERY TIME

### Objection 1: "What if the AI says something wrong to my customer?"

**Why they say it:** A contractor's reputation is built over years. One bad interaction with a homeowner they know personally — or who knows their neighbors — can cost them more than a year of service fees. They're not worried about software bugs. They're worried about embarrassment.

**The wrong response:** "The AI is very accurate. It's been trained on thousands of conversations." — This is abstract reassurance. It doesn't address the fear and invites skepticism.

**The right response:**

> "That's exactly why we don't flip it to autopilot on day one. In Week 2, it runs in what we call Smart Assist mode — the AI writes the response, but it waits five minutes before it sends. If you're available, you can read it and edit it. If you're on a roof, it sends without you. By the time we go fully autonomous in Week 3, you've already seen fifty responses and know exactly how it handles your customers.
>
> And when the AI doesn't know something — let's say a lead asks about a specific permit situation — it doesn't guess. It says something like 'Great question, let me have [your name] follow up on that directly.' We flag it, I look at it that day, and we add the answer to its knowledge base. It gets sharper every week.
>
> Can I show you what that actually looks like?"

Then pull up the conversation sample below and walk through it live.

**Proof point — Sample AI conversation (kitchen reno inquiry):**

> **Lead (Sarah T.):** Hi, I found your number online. We're looking to do a full kitchen renovation, probably $40-60k range. Are you taking on projects this spring?
>
> **[Business Name]'s assistant:** Hi Sarah, thanks for reaching out. Yes, [Business Name] is scheduling spring projects now. A $40-60k kitchen reno is definitely in our wheelhouse. To make sure we're a good fit, can I ask — are you in [service area], and do you have a rough timeline in mind?
>
> **Lead:** Yes, we're in [area]. Looking to start around May.
>
> **[Business Name]'s assistant:** Perfect. May works well. The next step would be a free estimate visit — [contractor name] comes out, walks the space, and gives you a detailed quote. It usually takes about 30-45 minutes. What does your schedule look like this week or next?
>
> **Lead:** We're pretty flexible. Thursday afternoons or Friday mornings work.
>
> **[Business Name]'s assistant:** Great. I have Thursday the 15th at 2pm or Friday the 16th at 10am available. Which works better?
>
> **Lead:** Thursday at 2 works.
>
> **[Business Name]'s assistant:** Confirmed — Thursday, [date] at 2pm. You'll get a reminder the morning of. If anything changes, just reply here. Looking forward to it.
>
> **Lead:** Quick question — do you do permits? Our last contractor skipped them and it was a nightmare.
>
> **[Business Name]'s assistant:** Really glad you asked that — it's an important one. I want to make sure [contractor name] gives you a complete answer on that directly, since permit requirements can vary by project scope. I'll flag this for him and have him reach out before the visit. Is this number the best way to reach you?

At message 6, the AI hit a boundary, said so honestly, and escalated without breaking the flow. Point this out to the contractor. This is the correction loop in action.

**Proof point — show the review window:**
If you have a live client on Smart Assist, pull up the conversation queue. Show a pending response with the 5-minute timer. Show the "edit and send" option. Make it concrete.

### Objection 2: "I tried a similar tool/agency before and got burned"

**Why they say it:** They spent money on GoHighLevel, an SEO agency, a chatbot, or an "AI assistant" that took 3 months and delivered nothing. They're not skeptical of this specific pitch — they're skeptical of the category. The fear is losing another $5,000-15,000 and having nothing to show for it.

**The wrong response:** "We're different. Our AI is better." — Every vendor says this. It confirms their pattern recognition, not contradicts it.

**The right response:**

> "Tell me what happened. What was it, and what specifically went wrong?"
>
> [Let them talk. Do not interrupt. You're listening for: software vs. managed service, upfront setup fee, contract length, what the product actually did vs. promised.]
>
> [After they finish:]
>
> "That makes sense. Here's the thing — what you're describing sounds like you bought software and they left you to figure it out. This is different in one specific way: I run it. I'm the one who sets it up, monitors it, fixes the KB when the AI gets something wrong, and sends you a report every two weeks showing exactly what happened. You don't manage anything.
>
> But honestly, I don't expect you to take my word for that. That's why the guarantee is the first thing I want you to look at, not the last. If you don't see 5 real leads engaged in your first 30 days — leads that replied to the system — you get your first month back. There's nothing to sign up for long-term. It's month-to-month.
>
> The question isn't whether you trust me yet. The question is: do you have 15-20 old quotes sitting there who never called you back? Because in the first week, we're going to text all of them. If even one of them responds, you've already covered the first month."

**Proof point:** Pull up Section 3 of the offer (guarantee terms). Read Layer 1 out loud — "5 Qualified Lead Engagements in your first 30 days or your first month is refunded." Then show Section 5 — "month-to-month, cancel anytime with 30 days' notice, no cancellation fees."

## TIER 2 — COMMON OBJECTIONS

### Objection 3: "I get most of my work from referrals — I don't have a lead problem"

**Why they say it:** They're telling the truth. Established renovation contractors in Alberta often run 60-80% referral-driven. Speed-to-lead doesn't resonate because they don't lose many leads to response time. They need a different value frame entirely.

**The wrong response:** "You're losing leads to contractors who respond faster." — This is the wrong pitch for this contractor. It implies their referral pipeline is fragile. It will feel like a criticism.

**The right response:**

> "Referrals are the best leads in the business — closed rate is through the roof. So let me ask you this: how many estimates did you send in the last 90 days that just went quiet? People who said they were interested, got your number from a friend, you quoted them, and then... nothing?
>
> That's where most of the money is sitting. The system's best job is following up on those estimates, not chasing cold leads. We send a sequence of four personalized follow-ups over two weeks. Most contractors tell me that 20-30% of those quiet quotes come back.
>
> On top of that — every time you finish a job, we automatically ask for a Google review with a direct link. Referral contractors build their business on word-of-mouth. More reviews accelerate that. Your Google profile becomes part of the referral chain.
>
> And we handle payment collection reminders. Deposit requests, final invoice follow-up. How many times have you had to chase someone for a check?
>
> This isn't about speed-to-lead. It's about the revenue that's already in your pipeline that's going quiet."

**Proof point:** Ask them to estimate how many sent quotes are sitting unresponded from the past 90 days. Walk through the ROI math: if they have 20 old quotes at an average value of $30k, and even 2 come back — that's $60k. One month of service is $1,000.

---

### Objection 4: "I already respond fast / I have someone answering calls"

**Why they say it:** They're proud of their responsiveness — and rightly so. Faster response is one of the most cited reasons contractors close more jobs. They hear "AI response" and assume it's a replacement for something they're already doing well.

**The wrong response:** "The AI responds in seconds, which is faster than any human." — This positions their assistant or their own responsiveness as inadequate. They'll get defensive.

**The right response:**

> "That's great — and it's exactly why your close rate is probably better than most contractors. Fast response matters. But let me ask you this: what happens Tuesday at 10am when you're on a job site and someone submits a form on your website? Or someone calls your cell, you don't recognize the number, you're mid-cut, you let it go to voicemail?
>
> The system isn't replacing [assistant name] or your phone habits. It covers the gaps — the three-hour window on a job site, the Saturday afternoon call, the form fill that comes in at 9pm. It also handles everything your assistant doesn't have time for: following up on every estimate, sending appointment reminders, collecting deposits, asking for reviews after every job.
>
> How many estimates has [assistant] followed up on in the last two weeks?"

**Proof point:** If they have a staff member, reframe explicitly around the follow-up and payment features. Offer to walk through the estimate follow-up sequence specifically — show the 4-touch sequence and the timeline.

## TIER 3 — LIKELY WITH SPECIFIC PROFILES

### Objection 5: "I don't want my customers talking to a robot"

**Why they say it:** They've had a bad chatbot experience personally — the kind that loops, gives wrong answers, and makes you feel like the company doesn't care. They associate "AI" with that experience.

**The wrong response:** "The AI sounds completely human — you can't tell the difference." — Misleading, and it raises the next question: "So you're tricking my customers?"

**The right response:**

> "It doesn't pretend to be you. Every message identifies itself as [Business Name]'s assistant. Homeowners know they're getting an automated response. The point isn't to fake it — it's to give them a real answer in under a minute instead of silence for three hours.
>
> What contractors tell me is: their customers actually prefer it. They get an immediate response at 9pm on a Saturday. They get appointment reminders. They get a payment link instead of an awkward check conversation. The experience feels professional, not robotic.
>
> The conversations that need a human — anything complex, anything with a frustrated customer, anything technical — those get escalated to you. The AI's job is the easy 80%. You handle the 20% that actually needs you."

**Proof point:** Show the sample conversation from Objection 1. Point out message 6 where the AI flags the permit question and escalates. That's the handoff in practice.

---

### Objection 6: "I'm too busy right now — I'll look at this in spring"

**Why they say it:** They're not actively lying — they probably are busy. But "spring" is a delay tactic. The real question is whether the timing urgency is real.

**The wrong response:** "Totally understand, I'll follow up in March." — You've just lost the conversation.

**The right response:**

> "I hear you. Can I ask — how many estimates are you sitting on right now that haven't come back? Because those don't wait for spring. If someone got three quotes in November and you haven't followed up, they've probably already made a decision.
>
> The best time to run the reactivation on those is right now, while they're still in the decision window. The busier you are, the more those follow-ups fall through the cracks.
>
> If timing is the issue, the setup takes one 30-minute call. Everything after that I handle. You don't take time away from jobs."

**Proof point:** Estimate follow-up ROI math. If they have 15 outstanding quotes at $25k average: at a 15% reactivation rate, that's $56k in recoverable pipeline. The system pays for itself many times over before spring arrives.

---

### Objection 7: "I already use Jobber/HubSpot — won't this overlap?"

**Why they say it:** They've invested in a tool and don't want to feel like they wasted money or add another login. They're worried about duplicate data and extra overhead.

**The wrong response:** "We integrate with everything." — Overpromise. The integration is real but specific — know what it does before making claims.

**The right response:**

> "Jobber is great for job management — scheduling, invoicing, crew coordination. This system sits in front of that. When a new lead comes in, texts the business number, or submits a form, that's where we work. We qualify them, book the estimate, follow up until they're ready. Once they're a confirmed job, they go into Jobber.
>
> And we do actually connect to Jobber now. When the system books an appointment, it fires that event to Jobber automatically so your jobs stay in sync. And when you mark a job complete in Jobber, we send the review request — so your review process is automatic, not manual.
>
> Think of it as the front end of your pipeline. Jobber handles the work. We handle getting you the work — and close the loop on reviews after."

**Proof point:** Walk through the lead pipeline stages on the dashboard. Show how a lead moves from new inquiry to estimate booked — the part that happens before any job management tool gets involved. Mention the `appointment_booked` event sync and the Jobber `job_completed` → review trigger as the specific integration points.

---

### Objection 8: "$1,000/month is expensive when I don't know if it works"

**Why they say it:** It's not actually about the price. It's about the risk-adjusted cost. If the system delivered $10k in recovered revenue every month, $1,000 would feel cheap. The real objection is uncertainty about ROI.

**The wrong response:** "Think about what you'd pay a full-time hire." — Comparison pricing doesn't address the fear of paying for nothing.

**The right response:**

> "Fair. Let me give you two numbers.
>
> One recovered kitchen project — the kind you already do — is probably $40,000 to $65,000. One project you'd have lost to slow follow-up pays for more than three years of this service.
>
> The more relevant number is your outstanding quotes. How many estimates have you sent in the last 90 days that went quiet? [Get the number.] Even if we get two of those back — what's the revenue?
>
> On the risk side: there's no contract. Month-to-month, cancel with 30 days' notice. And the guarantee: if you don't see 5 real leads engage in the first 30 days, you get that first month back. The risk on your side is one month."

**Proof point:** Run the ROI calculator live with the prospect's numbers. Ask: "How many leads do you get a month? What's your average project value? What percentage of quotes go quiet without a follow-up?" Then hit `POST /api/public/roi-calculator` with those numbers and show the output — annual revenue at risk and months to break even. If you're not at a computer, use the worksheet at `docs/operations/templates/REACTIVATION-ROI-WORKSHEET.md` and do it manually. At a 10% reactivation rate, one recovered project typically covers the entire year.

### Objection 9: "I don't have a lot of leads right now"

**Why they say it:** Often true in winter or during a slow stretch. They don't see the value if the system has nothing to respond to.

**The wrong response:** "The system will help you get more leads." — Out of scope and overclaims what the service does.

**The right response:**

> "Low inbound volume is actually the best argument for reactivation. Every estimate you've sent in the past year that didn't close — that's your pipeline. People who already know you, already got your number, already heard your name from someone they trust. We text all of them. Not a blast — personalized AI follow-up based on what they originally inquired about.
>
> That's not lead generation. That's recovering revenue that's already yours.
>
> And on the guarantee: if your lead volume runs below 15/month, the guarantee window extends proportionally. You're never penalized for a slow period."

**Proof point:** Ask how many quotes they've sent in the past 6 months. Most contractors can rattle off 10-30 names. Run the same ROI math as Objection 8.

---

### Objection 10: "My customers know me — I give them my personal cell"

**Why they say it:** Long-term clients text their cell directly. They value the personal relationship and worry that a business number feels corporate or creates a barrier.

**The wrong response:** "You should have boundaries. Keep your personal phone separate." — Sounds judgmental. They've built their business on personal touch and you're telling them they're wrong.

**The right response:**

> "Totally — don't change that. Existing clients can still reach you directly. This is for new inquiries: website forms, people who find you on Google, referred contacts who don't have your cell.
>
> The business number is separate. New leads come in there. The AI handles the first conversation, books the estimate. By the time you meet them, they've already been qualified and scheduled. You still do the relationship part.
>
> We can also set up call forwarding from the business number to your cell — so if someone calls the new number and it rings through, you can answer it yourself. The system only steps in if you don't pick up."

**Proof point:** Show the call forwarding configuration on the settings page. Make it clear the business number can be configured to behave exactly like their cell during their available hours — the AI only catches the gaps.

## SOCIAL PROOF FRAMEWORK

### The First Client Win

The most valuable thing you can collect is the story of a specific lead who went quiet, got a reactivation message, and became a booked job. You need this by end of Week 1 for the first client.

**When to capture it:** The moment a reactivated lead replies with interest — not when the job is won, but when the conversation restarts. That's the proof point. "This quote was three months old. They replied within an hour of the AI message."

**What to capture:**

- Lead first name (or initials — do not use full name without consent)
- Project type (kitchen, basement, bathroom — not address)
- Dollar value of original quote
- How long it had been sitting unresponded
- How quickly the lead replied after the system messaged them

**The story format (one paragraph, for use in proposals and verbal sales):**

> "One of our current clients had quoted a kitchen reno in January — $42,000, referred by a neighbor. The homeowner had gone quiet after getting three quotes. Two weeks into the service, the system sent a follow-up. The homeowner replied in forty minutes. The contractor had the job confirmed by end of week. That project alone covered the first year of service."

Adjust the numbers and project type based on what you actually capture. The structure is: quote was cold → system messaged → lead replied → contractor won the job.

**Asking for permission:**

> "Hey [contractor name] — the lead from the [project] quote just replied after the reactivation message went out. Would it be okay if I used that story (no names, just project type and dollar range) when I talk to other contractors? It's the best way to show how this works in practice."

Get a yes by text before using any specifics.

## QUIET HOURS POSITIONING

### Current State

The system enforces CRTC quiet hours: no commercial messages between 9 PM and 10 AM. Inbound inquiries received during that window are queued and sent at the next compliant window (10 AM).

There is code in the platform for an inbound-reply-allowed mode, but it is not active pending a legal opinion on whether direct replies to inbound inquiries qualify for exemption from CRTC quiet-hours restrictions. Do not promise or imply 24/7 response capability until that legal opinion is in hand.

### What to Tell Contractors

If a contractor asks about evening leads:

> "The system monitors around the clock. If someone submits a form at 9 PM on a Sunday, the system sees it immediately. The response goes out at 10 AM Monday morning — first thing in their inbox when they wake up. That's usually before any competitor has responded at all, even the ones with a human answering service.
>
> We're working through the compliance piece on true real-time evening responses. For now: evening form fills get a 10 AM response. Missed calls during quiet hours get a 10 AM text-back. No lead disappears."

### Script for "What about evening leads?"

> "Evening inquiries are queued and delivered at 10 AM — so they're the first thing the lead sees in the morning. In practice, that's still faster than most contractors who don't get to it until after their first job. The system is reviewing the legal basis for responding instantly to direct inbound messages during evening hours, but until that's confirmed, we're running conservatively on compliance. I'd rather be honest about that than overpromise and have a problem later."

### Recommendation

Get the CRTC inbound-reply exemption legal opinion before the second client conversation. The "evening form fills" question will come up in every sales call with a contractor who generates inbound leads from their website. The current interim answer is defensible but not a sales accelerant.

## Section 12: Outreach Scripts

**Before using any angle below:** Run the Pre-Sale Revenue Leak Audit for the prospect first (`docs/operations/templates/PRESALE-REVENUE-LEAK-AUDIT-TEMPLATE.md`). It takes 15-20 minutes using public data (Google Business Profile, website, competitor review counts) and gives you a specific data point — their review gap, their visible response time, their estimated dead-quote count — that makes every outreach angle more specific and harder to dismiss.

Use these angles for cold outreach (SMS, email, LinkedIn DM). Pick the angle that matches the prospect&apos;s profile from pre-qualification research.

**Pain hierarchy for angle selection:** Missed calls OPEN conversations (visceral, every contractor has THE story). Dead quotes CLOSE deals (concrete ROI, found money). Lead with missed calls on cold calls and texts. Pivot to quote reactivation once you&apos;re in a conversation. Use quote reactivation as the default for email/LinkedIn where you need a longer-form value prop.

### Angle A — Quote Reactivation (Default for email/LinkedIn)

**Best for:** Any contractor with an established business (3+ years). Lowest objection surface. Best for written outreach where you have space to make the ROI case.

> **Cold SMS/LinkedIn opener:**
> "Hey [Name] — quick question. How many quotes from the last 6 months are sitting in your phone with no response? Most renovation contractors I talk to have 15-30 dead quotes. We built a system that texts those people back automatically with personalized follow-up — and typically 20-30% respond. Would it be worth a 15-minute call to see if it makes sense for [Business Name]?"

> **Email subject:** "Your outstanding quotes"
> **Email body:**
> "[Name], I noticed [Business Name] does [kitchen/bathroom/basement] renovations in [City]. Quick question — how many estimates from the last 6 months never got a response? Most contractors in your space have $200K-500K in dead quotes sitting in their phone. We recover those. 15 minutes to show you how. Worth a call?"

**Conversation pivot (if they respond):** "What's your typical project value? And roughly how many quotes would you say went cold in the last 6 months?" Then do the math live: [quotes] x [value] x 15% recovery = pipeline recovered.

### Angle B — Referral-Heavy Contractor

**Best for:** Contractors who say "I get my work from referrals, I don't have a lead problem."

> **Opener:**
> "Hey [Name] — you probably don't have a lead problem. Most established contractors in [City] don't. But here's what I keep hearing: estimates go out, the homeowner goes quiet for 3 weeks, and nobody follows up because you're on a job site. The estimate follow-up alone — not lead gen — typically recovers 1-2 projects per quarter. Plus automated review requests after every job. Would a 15-minute call make sense?"

**Conversation pivot:** "How many Google reviews do you have right now? And how many estimates went out last month that you never heard back on?"

### Angle C — Speed-to-Lead

**Best for:** Contractors running Google Ads or getting web form leads. Lead volume is high, response time is slow.

> **Opener:**
> "Hey [Name] — saw [Business Name] is running Google Ads for [service]. Quick question: when a lead fills out your form at 8 PM on a Tuesday, how fast do they hear back? Industry data says the first contractor to respond wins the job 78% of the time. We respond in under 10 seconds, 24/7. Worth a 15-minute call?"

### Angle D — Seasonal Ramp

**Best for:** March-April outreach when contractors are entering busy season and can't keep up with inquiries.

> **Opener:**
> "Hey [Name] — spring is here and renovation inquiries are about to spike. Last year, most contractors in [City] missed 30-40% of inbound calls during peak season because they were on job sites. We make sure every call, text, and form fill gets an instant response — even when you're elbow-deep in a demo. 15 minutes to see if it fits?"

### Pre-Call: Lead Leak Snapshot

Before or during outreach, send the prospect a link to the Lead Leak Snapshot calculator on the marketing site. It asks 5 questions and outputs their personalized annual revenue at risk. This converts the conversation from &ldquo;is $1,000 expensive?&rdquo; to &ldquo;I&apos;m losing $X/year and this costs $12K.&rdquo;

If they complete it before the call, open with: &ldquo;I saw your numbers &mdash; you have about $[X] in dead quotes and another $[Y] in estimates with no follow-up. That&apos;s $[total] sitting on the table. Want to talk about getting some of it back?&rdquo;

If they haven&apos;t completed it, run through the 5 questions verbally during the call and do the math live with the ROI worksheet.

Spec for the calculator is at `active/consensus/roi-stress-test.md` (Lead Leak Snapshot section).

### Pre-Call Qualification (run before every sales call)

**Step 0 — Run the Pre-Sale Revenue Leak Audit (15-20 min, before you reach out):**
Using publicly available data only (Google Business Profile, website, Google Maps competitor check), complete the lightweight audit at `docs/operations/templates/PRESALE-REVENUE-LEAK-AUDIT-TEMPLATE.md`. This gives you a personalized opener: &ldquo;I did some research before reaching out — you have 47 Google reviews and your top competitor has 214. I can tell you what that gap is costing you.&rdquo; It also pre-qualifies the prospect (low review count, no quick response, many unresponded estimates visible on GBP = high fit).

Before the call, gather:
1. **Lead volume**: &ldquo;How many inbound inquiries do you get per month?&rdquo; (target: 10+)
2. **Outstanding quotes**: &ldquo;How many estimates are sitting with no response?&rdquo; (target: 5+)
3. **Google reviews**: Check their Google Business Profile review count vs. top 3 competitors (already done in pre-sale audit)
4. **Current tools**: &ldquo;Do you use Jobber, ServiceTitan, or anything for lead management?&rdquo;
5. **Guarantee window**: If below 15 leads/month, calculate the adjusted guarantee window and state it explicitly during the call: &ldquo;Your guarantee window would be X days, not 30, based on your lead volume.&rdquo;
6. **Loss ceiling**: State it plainly: &ldquo;Your maximum risk is $1,000. One month. If 5 leads don&apos;t engage in 30 days, you get it back. One project covers 3-5 years of the service.&rdquo;
7. **ROI Calculator**: Have the calculator ready at `POST /api/public/roi-calculator` or the ROI worksheet at `docs/operations/templates/REACTIVATION-ROI-WORKSHEET.md`. Enter their numbers live during the call if price comes up.
